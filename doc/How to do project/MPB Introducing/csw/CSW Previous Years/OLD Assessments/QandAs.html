LAST DAY FOR QUESTIONS: 18 JANUARY 2010

These are questions and answers about the CSW assessment for 2009-10.

Questions must be e-mailed to king@cs.york.ac.uk.

Questions will not be answered after Spr/2/Mon, Monday 18 January 2010.

Questions will always be answered on this page, not necessarily directly to the questioner.

Questions and their answers are given in reverse order of their answering. The dates are the dates that the answer is posted, not that when the question is asked. Questions will be made anonymous when transcribed here.

You may find it helpful to see questions that have been asked in previous years, when the rubrics have been very similar, except for the instructions about referencing style.

  • In the area of software test, I found two papers that related to mutation test. The one I found in HISE group has basically introduced mutation testing for OO programs, while another found in NSC group is how to evolve mutation testing by Artificial Immune System. Then I am going to find some ideas that associate natural algorithm with software safety or security. Could you please advice me whether this topic meet the requirements of CSW assessment?

Mutation Testing is clearly an area of interest to the groups mentioned, so this sounds fine.

  • I'm still unsure exactly what is meant by "You should select from the subject areas of the research groups, and the members of staff in the research groups, of the Department.". If I chose to do something related to the Computer Vision research group, then their research areas include pattern recognition and image processing, amongst others. Does this mean that it would be alright to do anything within the field of image processing, for example something to do with Optical Character Recognition (OCR)? This does not seem to be a particular research interest of anyone in the Computer Vision research group, and there are no publications on this topic by anyone in the group as far as I know. However, this still seems to be covered by the subject areas of the research group.

    I suppose I am just asking whether we are limited to a subject which has been explicitly explored by someone within the research groups (and publications on that specific subject exist within the department), or just the general subject areas of the research groups?

First: you are not restricted to publications by York people. (That would be very boring!) Second, the restriction on subject area is not intended to be very strict, but I do want to prevent people doing a lit review on 'What I did in my summer holidays' or some other esoteric subject unrelated to CS. The restriction to 'York research group subject areas' is simply meant to be an easy way to define 'sensible bits of Computer Science'. For your particular example, OCR seems a perfectly reasonable topic, even if no-one in CVPR group has explicitly published on it.

Spr/2/mon

  • Is it possible to submit a draft copy of the lit review for you to have a
look over and provide some general feedback about what is good/bad etc?

I understand if you don't want to commit to this but it would be handy to
know if I'm comparing the two papers enough and not just repeating what
they say. It all gets a bit fuzzy after you spend a lot of time reading
and writing about the same subject.

I'm afraid not. Firstly, if I did this for one student, I'd have to do it for all, and I simply don't have time for that. Secondly, commenting on drafts like this opens up all sorts of questions as far as parity of assessment is concerned: if one student's draft is pretty good and they don't need much assistance, while another's is not so good and they benefit greatly from the initial feedback, but the two assessments end up at the same level, is that fair? How can the achievement of the students and the assistance offered be factored into the mark awarded? We very seldom get involved in this sort of commenting on drafts for this reason. (Project reports themselves are an honourable exception!)

  • the assignment stated we should hand in no more than 6 A4 pages. Do the
cover page and the bibliography page count one of six?

The assessment (section 3.2) is pretty clear that the bibliography is included in the page count: "no more than 6 A4 pages, of which one A4 page shall comprise only the bibliography.". If you feel the need to add a cover page, I suppose I would probably be generous and not regard that as part of the page count.

Spr/1/mon

  • do we have to choose the literature written by the staff or the member of the research group of York?
No. It is the subject area that needs to be related to York research (see section 1.1 of the assessment paper). The choice of papers can be from anywhere (see section 1.2).

Spr/0/sat

  • I would like to choose 'comparison of concurrent programming languages' as
my review subject area. However, there is no publication on such topic in
any research group of York. I'm wondering if this meet the requirements of
the CSW assessment?

The assessment paper is quite clear on this point:

You should select from the subject areas of the research groups, and the members of staff in the research groups, of the Department.        [p2]

Thus, if you cannot find anything about concurrent programming languages among the work of the department's research groups, it does not meet the criteria. [But I'd be surprised, particularly if there's nothing in the PLASMA group's work.]. Sorry.

  • Is use of a 2-column format allowable or preferred, or should I stick
to the standard single-column article format?

The assessment paper is not so clear on this! There are no stated restrictions on the page layout to be used, so a 2-column format would be acceptable. However, please note that there are wordcount restrictions, as well as page count restrictions: if you want to use 2-column format to 'squeeze in' more material, make sure that the wordcount limits are not exceeded.

Aut/10/tue

  • Because I have no prior knowledge of what the review topic is, I'm finding
myself quoting lots of sentences from the chosen documents.

What I'm wanting to know is... if you state two sentences from the same
document (maybe from difference parts), in one paragraph - do you need to
add a citation at the end of every sentence or at the end of the whole
thing.

e.g.
Cars have four wheels [1]. Cars have a steering wheel [1].
OR
Cars have four wheels. Cars have a steering wheel [1].

Firstly, you need to make clear the extent of every quotation. However many citations you have, the above examples should have quotation marks to show how long the quotation actually is: the reader may infer that it ends where the citation is, but where does it start? [An alternative, with longer quotations, is to use indentation to set out clearly which is the quotation and which your own text. Or you might even get away with the use of italic, but I wouldn't recommend that!]

As for number of citations, I'm afraid you need one for each quotation. I suppose you could reduce it by using ellipses: "sentence 1 ... sentence 2" [1] , but I'd only do that if there was a small bit missed out, not, for example, if sentence 1 came from chapter 1 of a book and sentence 2 from chapter 7! The other thing I'd ask is whether you really need quotations, or could you paraphrase? And remember that you're going to get more credit for your analysis of the text, rather than for your choice of which sentences to quote.
