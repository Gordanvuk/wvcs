  • Whilst on the stage of formatting for this assessment I was wondering if there is a ready template that you would rather we use. We were prescribed a specific format and template for an assessment submitted earler.

    I am not aware of anything in the assesesment that implies a set style. (There is a warning that the Department's "project" style prepared By J. Jacob has a lot of white space in it!). However, please pay VERY careful attention to the length and structure set out in the assessment. As the assessment makes clear, these are mandatory, and marks will be lost for not following them.

  • Is it OK if the papers we review for the CSW assignment are not recent? I ask because review papers of the type we've been assigned are usually written about the current state of the particular research area, and I've found an interesting subject for which a large number of papers were written in the early to mid 90s. Would it be acceptable to write my review using some of these old papers? Question

    I am not aware of anything in the assesesment that requires only recent papers to be considered. It is worth noting, however, that since CS is a rapidly developing subject area, a review of early work would be expected (in general - ie not just in the context of the CSW assessment ) to justify the earliness as well as other aspects.

/Spr/1/Wed

  • I have selected a topic in which I was previously interested. At the time, I asked a member of staff for a list of papers that they thought would be relevant. Is it appropriate for me to use these in my assessment? Obviously there were many more papers than are required for the project, so I would be making my own selection from the list.

    This is appropriate. It is not unusual, when doing an academic literature review, to ask other people in the field what papers they recommend; it's actually not that different to consulting a lecturer's publications list on the web. The key thing is that you are making the academic judgement about which papers to include in your review.

    The requirements are precisely as stated in the assessment: ie, no cover page is required. If you include one, it will count towards page and word limits (as it would in the final project report).

  • Can my supervisor review my draft?

    Again, I've had several requests like this.

    As with all other assessments, it is not appropriate for your supervisor to comment on your submission.

    On the other hand, you could talk to your supervisor about what, in general terms, comprises a good literature review... just as, in earlier years, you could have asked a supervisor about general programming issues or testing issues during a programming assessment.

    I've tried to capture the essence of what I think a good literature review is in the way that I have written the assessment. If there are particular phrases of the assessment question/guidelines that are unclear, do mail about these!

/Aut/7/Fri

  • I seem to have quite a few big charts and tables taking up space in my CSW literature review. Is it possible to move them into an appendix? What are the restrictions regarding this?

    There is no formal provision for appendices, but clearly it would not be good style to have supplementary material "cluttering up" the text.

    My advice is as follows, and is based in part on the Handbook directions for appendix use in PR5:
    (a) Use a (marked) appendix if you need to, but keep within the page and word limit.
    (b) Make sure that the inclusion of "big charts and tables" is actually necessary for the review - would a smaller summary be appropriate, for example?
    (c) If you have diagrams or tables taken unaltered from a source, that you are just including to help the reader, and do not contribute otherwise to your review, put these in a clearly-designated "unmarked appendix", and do not include this in the page and word counts.

    Note that an appendix must be properly referenced, cited, and annotated, to avoid losing marks. Unmarked appendices must be properly cited.

/Aut/6/Tues

  • I have read some papers for another assessment: can I use the same papers for the CSW literature review?

    Yes, so long as the other assessment was not a literature review. It is the output that has to differ, not the inputs: there is a University guideline that the same material cannot be presented for two qualifications, but if you are using the papers for a different purpose, you are not in breach of this guideline. (By analogy, if you had two assessments on one module, or if your final project were on a topic also covered by a module, you would undoubtedly use some of the same sources, but you would be using them for a different purpose.)

/Aut/5/Fri

  • Is 'Artifical Immune Systems and their Use in Information Processing' too large a topic? or should it be 'The use of AISIID in web mining'?

    'Cryptographic APIs' or could it be larger 'An analysis of various methods of Crypotography and its Interfaces' or more specific 'Microsoft's Crypotgraphic APIs' (as they have a couple of varying versions, due to the new release of vista and .NET).

    I'm just trying to find something commercially practical, but within the scope of the project. I'd like it to be security systems based, but I'm not sure how deep into it you'd like.

    Can I turn the problem round, please. The assessment requires you to do and present a literature review. The marks are for review, not for subject area. If you can find two or three papers from which you can construct a coherent academic review, as described in the assessment, then you have found an aspect (focus) for your review; you also need some general stuff to reference when you introduce the subject, but that's unlikely to be a problem.

    The Microsoft topics are the only ones that I would have any concerns about: the issue here is not whether the subject/aspect is right, but whether there are two or three academic papers that can be used to construct a suitable review (it would not matter if introductory material were taken from more pop-press sources, but the review should be genuinely academic papers (eg. from conferences, academic journals, academic books etc).

    So, in short, don't worry about scope and depth; worry about whether you have suitable sources to review! Note that the marking scheme refers to features of academic review, not features of subject/content/depth/challenge etc.

/Aut/5/Thurs

  • I am having difficulty deciding on a subject area for my CSW assessment. The assessment guidlines read that I "must select a subject area". Does this mean an item from the list of research areas from a group, or can I cross research areas or even groups? These research areas are quite general, so should I write something that gives an overview of the area, or can I focus in on something more specific? Is it possible for me to ask you if you think a topic is appopriate, or would this violate the anonymity policy?

    I've had several mails on similar lines. I do not want to be over-restrictive on the area you chose. I am aware that some people already have a specific area of interest (some already have before they come here!); such people tend to have an idea of who is working in that area and where to look for papers. Other people may just have a general idea of what they might be interested in, and could use the department research pages to try to focus in and find papers.

    If you have a research interest that is not obviously covered by one of our research groups, or that cuts across research groups, and you are confident that you can identify an aspect and suitable papers, I would certainly not discourage you from picking that area!

    A good review of this length would typically need a fairly narrow focus (that is the aspect, referred to in the assessment), but I guess for many people they will have to arrive at that focus by working down from a more general idea - for instance, real time, to real time java, to ravenscar; natural computation to bio-inspired algorithms to ant navigation inspired... etc. (And, note, that it can be quite hard to construct a decent review if you pick two or three papers by the same authors, as they tend to take very similar standpoints etc).

    I am happy to answer (and record on this page) questions along the lines of "is this a suitable subject area" (the answer is likely to be yes, but, as you can see from the above, that would still mean that you need to find an aspect to review!). I am happy to answer questions along the lines of "does subject area z count as a research area, or is it just journalism"!

    I cannot answer questions on whether "aspect x" is suitable, or "papers a, b and c" are suitable. At the level of the papers and review focus, suitability is determined by how you go about the review, not an arbitrary decision by an outsider.
