 Exploring the factors associated with Web site success in the context of electronic commerce
Information & Management, Volume 38, Issue 1, October 2000, Pages 23-33
Chang Liu and Kirk P. Arnett

Abstract
Web sites are being widely deployed commercially. As the widespread use and dependency on Web technology increases, so does the need to assess factors associated with Web site success. The objective is to explore these factors in the context of electronic commerce (EC). The research framework was derived from information systems and marketing literature. Webmasters from Fortune 1000 companies were used as the target group for a survey. Four factors that are critical to Web site success in EC were identified: (1) information and service quality, (2) system use, (3) playfulness, and (4) system design quality. An analysis of the data provides valuable managerial implications for Web site success in the context of electronic commerce.

 Improving public relations web sites through usability research
Public Relations Review, Volume 27, Issue 2, Summer 2001, Pages 223-239
Kirk Hallahan

Abstract
Public relations communicators can use a variety of techniques to pretest the effectiveness of messages, including readability testing, focus groups, peer and jury reviews, experiments and field testing/test marketing. Another yet-untapped technique is usability research, a set of procedures used for more than 50 years in product and software development. Although usability is a technique that can be applied to any type of public relations message, usability research has particular relevance for enhancing the effectiveness of web sites and other new interactive techniques. This article examines the nature and value of usability research, and the elements of an effective web site based on usability principles. Applications to other types of public relations communications are also discussed.

Perceived disorientation: an examination of a new measure to assess web design effectiveness

Jaspreet S. Ahuja, a, 1 and Jane Webster, , b
a Solect Technology Group Inc., 55 University Avenue, Suite 200, Toronto, Ont. Canada M5J 2H7
b Queen's School of Business, Queen's University, Kingston, Ont. Canada K7L 3N6
Received 22 January 2001; accepted 26 February 2001 Available online 20 September 2001.

Abstract

In this paper, we present the development of a new measure of perceived disorientation that helps to explain experiences with Web-based systems. Two studies, incorporating over 300 participants, provide evidence for the measure's construct validity. The first study is a survey that develops this new measure and distinguishes it from ease of use. The second study, an experiment investigating users performing an information retrieval task on the Web, further distinguishes disorientation from ease of use, and relates it to actions. Moreover, the study compares the effects of Web designs containing simple and global navigation systems; these systems related to perceived disorientation but not to ease of use or actions. Finally, the study examines disorientation's relationship with user performance and demonstrates that perceived disorientation relates more strongly than actions to performance. Our perceived disorientation measure is simple and quick to administer to users, and we conclude by suggesting that designers will find it useful in assessing and comparing Web designs.


Author Keywords: Disorientation; Lost; Ease of use; Navigation; World Wide Web; Internet; Performance; Web design; Construct validity; Electronic commerce

Vitae

The World Wide Web (Web) represents a distributed hypermedia environment located on a global network of interconnected servers called the Internet (Hoffman et al., 1996). It can be described as a large database of interconnected hypertext documents, and browsing can be related to scanning the records of a database. The Web was introduced in 1991 and the number of hosts has since grown from 500 000 to over 93 million in 2000 (Internet Software Consortium, 2000). The promise of the Web is its ability to produce complex, richly-interconnected and cross-referenced bodies of multimedia information ( Utting and Yankelovich, 1989). Unfortunately, hypermedia also have the ability to produce complex, disorganized tangles of haphazardly-connected documents that make it very difficult to locate information ( Utting and Yankelovich, 1989). Thus, one of the most common problems faced by users navigating through hypertext is that of disorientation ( McDonald and Stevenson, 1998).

Disorientation, or the tendency to lose one's sense of location in a Web site, can cause users to become frustrated, lose interest, and experience a measurable decline in efficiency (McDonald and Stevenson, 1998). Even the most experienced Internet users often have difficulty in finding the products they want in virtual stores. According to a survey by Zona Research Inc. (1998), 28% of Internet-savvy users reported that it was either somewhat or extremely difficult to locate specific products and 62% gave up looking for products on line. Thus, the assessment of disorientation represents an important issue for Web designers.

Although disorientation represents an important issue, little empirical research has addressed this topic. When assessing disorientation, researchers have incorporated small sample sizes and generally have examined logs of users' actions when browsing in order to infer their levels of disorientation, rather than measuring their feelings (e.g. McDonald and Smith). In contrast, in two studies incorporating over 300 participants, we develop a perceptual measure of user disorientation, relating it to actions, Web designs, and performance and distinguishing it from a related construct, perceived ease of use.

The remainder of this paper proceeds as follows. We begin by describing Study 1, a survey study concerned with the development of our perceived disorientation measure. We then present Study 2, an experiment that continues the examination of the construct validity of our disorientation measure by distinguishing it from ease of use, relating it to Web designs and user performance, and demonstrating that it predicts performance better than actions. The paper concludes by presenting implications for practice and research.

1. Study 1: Development of a perceived disorientation measure

Disorientation has been defined as the tendency to lose one's sense of location and direction in a non-linear document (Conklin and Head. Woods (1984) called it the ‘getting lost’ phenomenon and suggested that it occurs when “the user does not have a clear conception of relationships within the system, does not know his present location in the system relative to the display structure, and finds it difficult to decide where to look next within the system” (pp. 229–230). Similarly, Smith (1996) called it the ‘lost in hyperspace phenomenon’ that occurs when users experience “cognitive problems of finding their way in the information space” (p. 365). It is the feeling experienced by users who do not know where they are within hypertext documents or how to move to desired locations.

Disorientation has led Web users to make such remarks as “I had specific places I wanted to go and couldn't understand how to find them” (Jarvenpaa and Todd, 1997, p. 74). When disoriented, users might miss certain sections of text, causing them to re-open the same page (a single hypertext document in the Web site) again and again. For instance, Web user surveys have demonstrated such disorientation issues as difficulties in: finding pages known to exist, finding a page already visited, and visualizing paths taken and paths that could be taken ( Pitkow and Kehoe, 1996).

In order to assess whether Web sites disorient users, researchers have examined actions, such as the number of pages (nodes) chosen and the number chosen more than once (e.g. McDonald and Smith). However, these behaviors are indirect measures of disorientation and results are interpreted based on actions rather than on what they actually feel. Although some have recommended that disorientation should be assessed as a degradation in user performance rather than as user feelings, utilizing actions as proxies for disorientation can be problematic: for instance, users who tend to explore a Web site will be rated as disoriented, even though they may be experiencing no disorientation ( Smith, 1996).

In contrast to capturing actions, some research has suggested that feelings may relate more directly to outcomes than their actions will. For instance, Otter and Johnson (2000) examined users retrieving information from a Web site. They found no correlation between any ‘disoriented’ actions and the accuracy of mental models of the Web site; however, they did find that those reporting low ‘lostness’ on a one-item perceptual measure did produce more accurate mental models. Further, they found a strong correlation between perceived lostness and performance (task time) but a non-significant correlation between actions and performance. This research suggests that a perceived measure may be a more appropriate indicator of disorientation — and this assertion is consistent with researchers in the behavioral sciences who suggest that to understand internal feelings, questionnaire measures represent more suitable methods of measurement ( Sandelands and Buckner, 1989). As a search of the literature found no existing valid perceptual measures, we developed a new measure of perceived disorientation.

Disorientation may appear conceptually similar to ease of use, or the “effort one experiences in the process of carrying out tasks using a given system” (Davis et al., 1992, pp. 1114–1115). In support of this, Head et al. (2000) modified Davis' (1989) ease of use measure to be Web-specific, and included one item, ‘confusing’, that could relate to disorientation. Similarly, Otter and Johnson (2000) included one item measuring perceived ‘lostness’ with a series of Web-specific ease of use items, and found that lostness correlated with ease of learning the system and with ease of finding information. Thus, an empirical question concerns the relation between these two constructs: can one discriminate between disorientation and ease of use? Therefore, as described next, we also examine the discriminant validity of disorientation, that is, whether disorientation can be differentiated from related constructs, such as ease of use ( Ghiselli et al., 1981).

1.1. Method

An initial measure of disorientation was constructed based on both the literature that describes the construct (i.e. Darken and Sibert, 1993; Denis; McDonald; Thorndyke; Thorndyke and Woods) and the input of ten graduate students, most of whom were studying information systems. First, the ten graduate students were given a definition of disorientation based on the literature, and then were asked to generate items. Next, we filtered the items to remove duplicates and others that did not relate directly to disorientation. This first set of questions was then pre-tested on another set of ten graduate students who also gave feedback on their wording. After incorporating their suggested changes, dropping items with low internal-consistency reliabilities (i.e. items that did not reflect the same construct: Trochim, 1999), and adding new items that we created, a final pretest was conducted on a third set of ten graduate students. With this third set of students, the comments were minimal, mostly directed towards the format and layout of the measure. After minor modifications based on the final pretest and again dropping items with low internal-consistency reliabilities, a twelve-item measure was developed (the twelve items loading on factors 1, 3, and 4 in Table 1, above).

We then assessed the 12-item measure's internal-consistency reliability and discriminant validity with a larger sample. For this study, 135 undergraduate and graduate students were drawn from three courses at a North American university (Organizational Behavior, Engineering Economics, and Human–Computer Interaction). About 60% of the participants were in the age group of 17–20 and around 84% were 24 or below. Most of the participants were male (70%), undergraduate students (84%), from the Engineering school (79%), with others from Applied Health Sciences, Arts, and Math. Most of the participants (90%) had spent at least one year using the Internet, which is normal for their age group (Pitkow et al., 1998).

Participants completed a questionnaire containing disorientation, ease of use, and demographic questions. To ensure that participants responded concerning a specific web site (and not in general) the survey stated: “Please think of the last web site you browsed. Then answer the following questions with respect to that particular web site only”. Participants responded to the disorientation items on seven-point scales ranging from ‘never’ to ‘always’. To assess ease of use, participants responded to three items adapted from Davis (1989) measure, such as ‘Learning to use the site was easy’, on seven-point scales ranging from ‘strongly disagree’ to ‘strongly agree’. Internal-consistency reliability (Cronbach's alpha) for ease of use was 0.87.

1.2. Results

An exploratory factor analysis using varimax rotation was conducted for the ease of use and disorientation items. Four factors emerged, with seven of the disorientation items loading on factor 1, the three ease of use items loading on factor 2, and the remaining disorientation items loading on factors 3 and 4 (see Table 1). The five disorientation items loading on factors 3 and 4 were dropped (because they did not load with the other disorientation items — they appeared to assess other constructs, like confidence, or cross-loaded on several factors). Our disorientation measure was constructed from the seven items loading on factor 1 (with a Cronbach's alpha of 0.89).

These factor analysis results support the discriminant validity of disorientation, as the ease of use items loaded separately from the disorientation items. However, the correlation between our disorientation measure and ease of use was high (r=−0.52, p<0.001), suggesting a close relationship between the constructs. It may be that they are related, yet conceptually distinct, constructs.

Our disorientation measure represents one of the first perceptual measures of disorientation. Future research should continue to examine the relationship between this multi-item perceived disorientation measure and both ease of use and related constructs. Thus, in Study 2, we continued to assess the construct validity of our perceived disorientation measure by relating it to ease of use, actions, Web designs, and user performance.

2. Study 2: an experiment examining the relationship of disorientation to web designs and user performance

In this study, we extend our assessment of the construct validity of our perceived disorientation measure. As described next, the process for assessing construct validity includes such evaluations as content validity, discriminant validity, concurrent validity, and predictive efficacy.2

Content validity concerns whether a measure represents the content domain of the construct (Trochim, 1999). Specifically, the seven disorientation items (presented as Factor 1 in Table 1, above) can be compared against the definition of disorientation presented earlier, that is, the situation in which ‘the user does not have a clear conception of relationships within the system, does not know his present location in the system relative to the display structure, and finds it difficult to decide where to look next within the system’ ( Woods, 1984, pp. 229–230). A close match between the items and Wood's definition is seen, supporting the content validity of the measure.

An assessment of discriminant validity should demonstrate that perceived disorientation diverges from related constructs (Ghiselli et al., 1981). Here, we examine the relation of perceived disorientation to actions and perceived ease of use. First, we expect perceptions and actions to relate weakly. As described earlier, actions may be influenced by factors in addition to disorientation, such as exploratory behaviors ( Smith, 1996). In support of this, Otter and Johnson (2000) found only a marginally-significant correlation between their one-item perceived measure of lostness and actions. Second, we re-examine the relationship between disorientation and ease of use and, as in Study 1, expect the constructs to be related but to load on separate factors. Thus, we propose that:

Hypothesis 1.  The perceived disorientation measure will demonstrate discriminant validity. Specifically, (a) perceived disorientation and actions will relate positively but weakly, and (b) perceived disorientation and ease of use will be distinct, but negatively-related, constructs.

Concurrent validity is assessed by examining whether a construct relates as expected to other constructs in its nomological network — that is, to constructs to which it should relate based on theory (Cronbach and Meehl, 1955). Concurrent validity is assessed when these constructs are measured at the same point in time ( Ghiselli et al., 1981). Here, we relate perceived disorientation to both Web designs and user performance on an information retrieval task.

In terms of Web designs, we propose that users interacting with a Web site containing a well-designed global navigation system will experience less disorientation than those interacting with a simple system. Navigation systems, or systems that support users in determining paths to be traveled through Web sites, may include features such as links to other pages, search facilities, directories, or site maps. Simple navigation systems present local links to other pages in the site (such as previous and next), while global navigation systems provide overviews of the entire site (such as site maps) on each page (Park and Kim, 2000).

Global navigation systems provide a visual representation of the entire system and we suggest that these visual representations should reduce cognitive overload, that is, the additional effort and concentration necessary to maintain several tasks or trails at one time (Conklin, 1987). The use of anchors, or invariant features of the Web site (whose identity and location is always prominently highlighted on successive displays, such as site maps that are always visible), will help to reduce the load on users' cognitive resources ( Tognazzine, 1999; Wise and Debons, 1987 and Woods). Therefore, global navigation systems should aid users in recognizing their positions, thereby helping to decrease cognitive overload and disorientation. In contrast, simple navigation systems provide fewer site representations; in this case, mental resources that are available for comprehension and understanding of material on the Web site will be reduced because users will need to allocate additional effort with regard to orientation and navigation ( Thuring et al., 1995). This will result in higher cognitive loads and disorientation for simple than for global navigation systems.

In support of the disorientation-performance link, McDonald and Stevenson (1998) suggested that disoriented users become frustrated, lose interest, and decline in efficiency. Further, Otter and Johnson (2000) found that perceived lostness related to mental model accuracy and task time on a Web information retrieval task. Thus, we propose that performance can be predicted by their perceived disorientation. In sum, we suggest that:

Hypothesis 2.  The perceived disorientation measure will demonstrate concurrent validity. More specifically, for users performing a Web information retrieval task: (a) a well-designed global navigation system will result in lower perceived disorientation than a simple navigation system, and (b) lower perceived disorientation will result in higher performance.

Finally, we examine the predictive efficacy, or incremental validity, of disorientation. Predictive efficacy represents the incremental predictive power of our measure (disorientation) as compared with other measures (Sechrest, 1963). That is, if disorientation shows stronger relationships with outcomes than other predictor variables (such as actions), this lends support to the predictive efficacy of our construct. In support of this, Otter and Johnson (2000) found that actions did not relate to mental model accuracy or performance. Thus, we propose that perceived disorientation will predict performance better than actions:

Hypothesis 3.  The perceived disorientation measure will demonstrate predictive efficacy. More specifically, perceived disorientation will predict performance on a Web information retrieval task better than actions.

2.1. Method

The hypotheses were tested in an experiment with multiple versions of the same on-line learning Web site (part of a larger study). In the experiment, participants searched for specific information on the Web sites, that is, they conducted formal searches. Data were collected by interaction logs, in which user interactions were automatically collected by the server software, by performance on a formal search, and by a post-experimental questionnaire.

The Web site used for the experiment was adapted from an on-going technical writing course offered on the Web by a North American university. This course, designed to teach principles of technical writing to non-technical students, included complete books on various aspects of technical writing. For the experiment, the amount of content was reduced by deleting sub-sections and chapters, resulting in three hierarchical levels; however, this had no impact on the look and feel of the site.3 In the course, users were first presented with an opening graphical view of their virtual office and presented with various options such as communication, assignments, bookshelf, information, and calendar.

2.2. Participants

Graduate and undergraduate students from various departments at a North American university acted as participants. The only prerequisite for participation was that the students needed to be knowledgeable about using computers and familiar with the World Wide Web. Participants were recruited by making brief presentations in courses (with more than 100 students) and requesting participation. Students interested in participating were asked to sign up and this was followed by an e-mail confirming their participation. Around 1200 students were approached in classrooms, 275 signed up, and 207 participated in the experiment. To make the task more realistic, we made the deliberate decision to pay participants, rather than offering them incentives based on performance — offering incentives would be inconsistent with students learning from a Web-based system or with customers looking for merchandise on a Web site. The participants were paid $10 each.

About 56% of the participants were in the age group of 17–20 and around 90% were 24 or below. Most of the participants were female (60%), undergraduate students (80%), from the Arts school (36%), with others from Applied Health Sciences, Engineering, Math, and Sciences. Most of the participants (88%) had spent at least one year using the Internet, again normal for their age group (Pitkow et al., 1998). Over 80% had more than nine years of education in the English language. Approximately 57% of the participants ranked themselves as novices with respect to their knowledge of Web design.

2.3. Procedure

The experiment was conducted in sessions ranging from one to 30 students (the computer lab in which the experiment was conducted had a capacity of 32). Students were randomly assigned to one of three versions of the same Web site. All sessions were conducted in the same computer lab at the same time of day. Further, the Web versions were placed on a local server, reducing variance in Web page retrieval times. Each session lasted almost 1 h. Experimenters (two graduate research assistants hired to conduct the experiments) conducted all sessions of the experiment. A script was used by the experimenters to give instructions to the participants regarding the purpose of the study and the details of the task.

At the beginning of each session, participants were given handouts that included brief information regarding the study. The participants were then instructed to turn on their monitors and navigate the Web site, finding answers to specific questions printed on a blue sheet (before the session, we had loaded the Web site onto their computers, and had turned off their monitors). They were asked to start finding answers immediately, and were told to take the most direct route possible to locate each answer, thereby providing few opportunities for exploratory behaviors. (We had blocked the sites' Web addresses to participants, minimizing the chances that they would surf elsewhere during the experimental sessions.) It was reasoned that in real-world e-commerce search situations, people trying to find information on a Web site would not spend much time familiarizing themselves with the site, but would directly start looking for what they wanted — i.e. they would be performing ‘formal’ searches (Choo et al., 2000). A maximum of 30 min (a conservative estimate based on two pilot experiments) was allowed to find answers to the six questions. The participants wrote their answers on the blue answer sheet when found. While the participants were browsing, a back-end automated program recorded their actions.

At the end of 30 min, participants were asked to stop browsing even if they were unable to find answers to all questions. (An alternative would have been to let participants take as long as they wanted — however, our performance measure then would have meant little. Therefore, we made the choice of a fixed amount of time for browsing. This is consistent with previous research, such as Head et al. (2000), who asked participants to find answers to precise questions under a time constraint.) Next, participants were given a post-experimental survey that measured their perceptions of ease of use and disorientation.

2.4. Measures

Perceptual disorientation measure. The 7-item measure developed in Study 1 was utilized, and demonstrated a Cronbach's alpha of 0.90.

Ease of use. The 3-item measure from Study 1 was utilized, and demonstrated a Cronbach's alpha of 0.86.

Disoriented user actions. Log analysis software captured ‘disoriented’ user actions. This program tracked usage patterns and needed to be developed in-house: an array of log analysis software available in the market was tested and none of them could produce the non-standard report we required (for user actions suggesting disorientation).4

According to McDonald and Stevenson (1998), the number of pages (nodes) opened and the number of pages opened more than once assess the level of disorientation of the user. (Similarly, Smith (1996) created a ‘lostness’ indicator based on the number of pages opened and the number of different pages opened. Further, Otter and Johnson (2000) extended Smith's measure by taking into account the type of link between Web pages, and found a correlation of 0.96 between their measure and Smith's measure.) Thus, the log analysis software captured these two types of indicators.

Web designs. We compared two (of the three) versions of the same Web site present in the larger study. These versions differed only in terms of navigation systems (that is, the content of the Web site was held constant). More specifically, we compared a simple navigation system and a well-designed global navigation system.5 The simple navigation system contained simple links, that is, links to the following page and to lower-level sections. The global navigation system allowed participants to move through documents by clicking on links and navigating in a hierarchy. It was text-based and was available on all pages. Additionally, it included backtrack facilities on all pages, cross-linking in certain locations, as well as search forms and site-maps. The global navigation system was always visible on the left of every page and was in the form of a collapsible tree structure (similar to the Explorer™ view in Windows™). Further, it was evaluated by our university's local Association for Computing Machinery (CHI) Computer–Human Interaction Group for consistency with usability guidelines; any changes recommended by the Group were implemented before conducting the experiment.

Performance. Performance was determined by the number of questions answered correctly during the formal search. To determine the number of correct answers, participants were presented with a blue sheet during browsing on which they were asked to find the answers to six questions, such as “If you wanted to know what the instructor has asked you to do, which is the best place to look: the Newsgroup or Instructor's comments?”. These six questions were pretested in the pilot experiments.

2.5. Results

Before presenting the results of the construct validity tests for disorientation, we must first present evidence that the measure demonstrates good distributional qualities. That is, the mean and median should be similar, skewness should be less than 2, kurtosis should be less than 5 (Ghiselli et al., 1981; Kendall and Stuart, 1958), and internal-consistency reliability should be high (generally above 0.80: Nunnally, 1978). These assessments were met: the mean and median were similar (3.09 and 2.86, respectively), skewness was 0.62, kurtosis was 0.01, and, as reported above, internal-consistency reliability was 0.90.

To examine the discriminant validity of disorientation (Hypothesis 1), we compared perceived disorientation with actions and ease of use. For Hypothesis 1a, we correlated perceived disorientation with actions and found weak relations: r=0.20 (p<0.01) for pages opened, and r=0.15 (p<0.05) for pages opened more than once. Thus, users' actions appear not to be substitutes for perceived disorientation, supporting Hypothesis 1a.

To examine Hypothesis 1b, we conducted a confirmatory factor analysis of the perceived disorientation and ease of use items, using structural equation modeling (SEM) with AMOS ( Arbuckle and Wothke, 1999). To evaluate fit, χ2/df (chi-square to degrees of freedom ratio), (AGFI) goodness of fit index, and (RMSEA) root mean square error were included. According to Hair et al. (1995) chi-square to degrees of freedom ratios less than 5, goodness of fit indices greater than 0.90, and RMSEA indices less than 0.08 indicate a good fit.

Fig. 1 and Table 2 present the results of the confirmatory factor analysis. Two competing a priori models are compared in Table 2. The first model tests the hypothesis that disorientation is distinct yet correlated with ease of use (that is, the model presented in Fig. 1). The second model examines whether disorientation and ease of use are a unitary construct; this allows us to compare the goodness of fit of the two models using a chi-square difference test for nested models ( Hair et al., 1995). If the two-factor model fits the data better and the chi-square difference test is significant, then this provides support for the discriminant validity of disorientation.

Supporting Hypothesis 1b, a comparison of the two models suggests that the two-factor model is a better fit: the two-factor model fits the data well (χ2/df=1.83, AGFI=0.91, RMSEA=0.06) while the one-factor model exhibits a much lower degree of fit (χ2/df=5.61, AGFI=0.73, RMSEA=0.15). A test of the chi-square difference between the two models (χ2 difference=126.62, p<0.001) supports this conclusion. Further, examining the critical ratio (CR) between disorientation and ease of use demonstrates that they are significantly correlated (r=−0.74, CR=−7.33).

Taken together, the results for actions and ease of use support the discriminant validity of perceived disorientation. Further, a comparison of the correlation of the Web versions (navigation systems) with disorientation (r=−0.30, p<0.001), actions (r=−0.03 and −0.01, for pages opened and pages opened more than once, respectively), and ease of use (r=0.06), point to the utility of the perceived disorientation measure for differentiating between Web designs.

To examine Hypothesis 2 concerning the concurrent validity of disorientation, we related the Web versions (navigation systems) to perceived disorientation as well as perceived disorientation to performance (controlling for Internet experience). First, a multiple regression of navigation system and Internet experience with disorientation was conducted (testing Hypothesis 2a). Results indicate that the navigation systems related to disorientation as expected (β=−0.30, p<0.001): mean disorientation was 3.40 for simple and 2.64 for global navigation systems. Second, a multiple regression of disorientation and Internet experience on performance was conducted (testing Hypothesis 2b). Results indicate that disorientation related negatively to performance (β=−0.27, p<0.001). In sum, these results support Hypothesis 2.

Hypothesis 3 compared the predictive efficacy of perceived disorientation with users' actions. Users' actions did not correlate with performance (r=0.01 and r=0.00, for pages opened and pages opened more than once, respectively), while perceived disorientation did (reported above for Hypothesis 2b). Therefore, users' actions do not predict performance as well as perceived disorientation, suggesting that perceived disorientation has higher predictive efficacy, thereby supporting hypothesis 3. 6

3. Discussion and conclusions

These studies represent a first effort at developing a perceptual measure of disorientation. This measure, based on the literature and created after a series of pretests, demonstrated good reliability. Further, our survey study suggested that it is different from a related construct, ease of use, and our experiment provided further support for its construct validity.

In our research, perceived disorientation related to differing Web designs (navigation systems) while both ease of use and users' actions did not differentiate between these Web designs. This suggests that the measurement of perceived disorientation by Web designers could provide a useful addition to current methods of assessing the usability of new Web designs and comparing the efficacy of current designs. The measure can be easily completed by users in only a few minutes, and is much simpler to collect and interpret than users' actions or the more time-consuming verbal protocols or videos of users' interactions.

This said, much work remains to be done in terms of validating this construct. For instance, it could be compared to a related construct, Park and Kim's (2000) ‘navigation convenience’ measure, that includes three questions assessing ease of navigation, ease of locating items, and ease of comparing multiple items. Further, Conklin (1987) proposed that the two main problems associated with Web browsing are disorientation and cognitive overload, yet we did not measure cognitive overload. Future research should investigate the relation of disorientation to cognitive overload during Web browsing, using such measures as the one developed by Perera (2000). Thus, future research should continue to examine the relationship between our multi-item perceived disorientation measure and both ease of use and related constructs.

Although perceived disorientation related to differing Web designs in our study, while ease of use did not, we do not mean to suggest that ease of use will be less important in assessing Web designs. In contrast, ease of use will continue to remain a key construct for the evaluation of information systems. We view disorientation as an additional assessment tool for the designer.

We found that perceived disorientation related weakly to users' actions thought to reflect disorientation (that is, the number of pages opened and those opened more than once) and that users' actions did not relate to performance. However, other user actions could be investigated in future research. For instance, the log files of those high and low in perceived disorientation could be compared for differing patterns of actions. Or, verbal protocols (Nisbett and Wilson, 1977) might help to elicit those actions that reflect perceived user disorientation.

In this study, we focused on formal searching of the Web because of its applicability to commercial applications such as electronic shopping. However, as Choo et al. (2000) suggested, users utilize one of four distinct modes when searching for information on the Web, undirected viewing, conditioned viewing, formal searching, or informal searching. Choo et al. found that informal searching, or a relatively limited and unstructured effort, represented the most frequent search mode for employees actually using the Web in their jobs. Thus, future studies extending this research to workplace applications should examine other modes of search, and especially informal search behaviors. In this case, we would expect to see an even weaker relationship between perceived disorientation and users' actions as many more exploratory (but not necessarily disoriented) behaviors would occur during informal searches ( Smith, 1996).

Designers have suggested that well-designed navigation systems can help to solve the disorientation problem experienced with hypertext (Fillion and Boyle, 1991) and our study results support this assertion. However, little research has addressed navigation support ( Park and Kim, 2000) and future research should continue to respond to calls for more research aimed at understanding Web behaviors (e.g. Perera, 2000) and specifically the effects of Web navigation systems on disorientation ( Otter and Johnson, 2000). Further, in this study we examined university students' reactions to differing navigation systems. Future studies should extend this research to other types of users.

Disorientation represents one of the most common problems faced by Web users. If disorientation leads to frustration and abandonment, then the measurement of perceived disorientation should be a key issue to consider when developing commercial Web sites. Lost clients can result in reduced revenues for organizations and ensuring that clients do not become disoriented should represent an important usability issue for designers. In conclusion, the study of perceived disorientation represents a rich source for future research that has wide-ranging practical implications.
