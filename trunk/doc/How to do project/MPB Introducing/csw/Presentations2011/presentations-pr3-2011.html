Computer Science Writing (CSW)
Presentations

All students taking PR3/PR4 will make an unassessed presentation in Autumn week 3. 3rd year MEng students and MSc students will do assessed presentations in the spring term. The remainder of this page applies to PR3/PR4 students.

PR3/PR4 student presentations

The timetabled slots and the group allocation for the presentations are given on the main CSW webpage. If you have problems with the timetable, please find a student in another session to swap with, and let me know by email of the changes by Friday 21st (6.00 pm).

Each student will make a 8 minute presentation, using a set of PowerPoint (or other application) slides. They may also give the audience any material on paper they think would be useful for the audience to look at during the presentation (this is unlikely, but if you have a table or something that is too detailed to present as a slide). At the end of each presentation there will be 2 minutes for questions and discussion.

The audience will be the lecturer, 1 or 2 demonstrators, and the other students in the group.

Students should email their slides to Stefano Pirandola pirs@cs.york.ac.uk at least 24 hours before their presentation slot, so we can be sure that they will run. The email should also state which session the slides are for.

Presentation Plan and Timings

The presentation should review one key paper (or at most two) from your literature review. The presentation itself should have the following four elements:

  1. Paper details: author, title, publication details
  2. Context: a brief statement of the area of computer science, and general background to the paper
  3. Content: what the paper covers
  4. Critical evaluation: merits and demerits of the paper, concluding comments

Content review should outline the underlying theme(s) of the paper, and perhaps illustrate a small example of what is covered.

Evaluation should contain critiques of the paper - can be positive or negative, what is most interesting/wrong etc with the paper. Is it advanced/trivial; easy read/unnecessarily complex etc....who should use the paper, and for what. Is the paper useful for further advances in the field?

As a rough guide, the paper details can be just displayed (at most 1 minute); the context should take 2 minutes; the content should take about 3 minutes, and the evaluation about 2 minutes.

Note that this structure suggests a few slides (roughly one per minute).

At the end of the presentation, there will be a couple of minutes for questions from lecturer/demonstrators and possibly from the audience too.

Presentation Feedback

The presentation is not formally assessed, but completed feedback sheets will be returned to students.
